‚è≥   Infinite Possibilities

For any decision you come across, it's highly likely that you're not exploring nearly enough and aggressively as you should be. The sad truth is that most of us don't realize how badly we're failing at exploration. If we could see a version of ourselves that had been exploring as much as we should, we would immediately see the vast difference (i.e. loss aversion).

People always say to explore more, but the crucial problem is that the correct level of exploration isn't obvious - whether it's figuring out what field we enjoy studying, what kind of friends we like to be around, what career to take, or how to solve a relationship problem. It's my goal to provide a more accurate framing of this issue.

Think about it this way: there are more possible positions in chess than atoms in the universe. There are more possible positions in Go than atoms in the universe. And yet, our lives are even more complex than these games. If you don't believe me, then either you've exhausted all the possible decision paths you could've tried, or you lack imagination. Sadly, the latter is probably true.

To give you an analogy, some of you might be familiar with the multi-armed bandit problem. For those who don‚Äôt know what that is, a ‚Äúsingle-armed bandit‚Äù is just a slot machine. If you pull on the lever, you will earn an unknown reward with an unknown probability. The multi-armed bandit problem is when you have many single-armed bandits with varying unknown rewards and unknown probabilities. In other words, a bunch of slot machines where you have no clue what the rewards are for each of them and how often they each come out. What should your strategy be to maximize the reward you can get?

This boils down to a problem of exploration (how much time is dedicated to trying different slot machines) vs. exploitation (how much time is dedicated to using the one slot machine that seems to give the best reward right now). In reality, life looks more like an infinite number of slot machines, each offering an infinite number of rewards depending on how you pull the lever. In other words, the number of possible actions you can take is so absurdly high that, even with 8 billion people in the world, you can be unique really easily.

With such a large number of slot machines and ways to pull on the machines, you would intuitively expect that the ‚Äúoptimal‚Äù strategy would include 1.5 metric shit-tons of exploring - after all, you have an infinite number of slot machines! Do you still really think you‚Äôre even remotely close to what you should be doing? If you do, then you probably lack imagination.

It‚Äôs not your fault that you have such a heavy bias toward exploitation. Specialization and following the status quo are woven so deeply into the fabric of modern reality that we don‚Äôt even realize it.

We‚Äôre expected to pick one field to major in and do a job related to it before you‚Äôre 20. We‚Äôre expected to work at just one company for a long time to ‚Äúclimb the ladder‚Äù. We‚Äôre expected to learn an instrument and stick with it for many years. We‚Äôre expected to solve math equations in one specific way or else we get a bad grade. We‚Äôre expected to pick one sport in the beginning. We‚Äôre expected to learn about school subjects in a really specific, arbitrary way that is uninspiring. We‚Äôre expected to go through middle and high school in a one-size-fits-all manner. Wow - so much for having an infinite number of slot machines!

Life, which is supposed to be beautifully creative, full of vibrant colors and emotions, imbued with curious paradoxes and mind-boggling mysteries, and an ever-changing dance between the border of order and chaos, has been unfairly reduced to a constant hum of mindless digging, black and white thinking, and lack of appreciation for the infinite potential that lies within us.

We have to embrace the full potential of our minds. We must strive to explore new ideas and try new things. This means taking risks, making mistakes, and facing challenges head-on. It means breaking out of the mold and embracing the unknown. It means embracing the beauty of life and all its complexities. It means striving to be the best versions of ourselves, not just for our own sake, but for the sake of those around us.

When was the last time you explored a completely orthogonal topic, just for the sake of curiosity? When was the last time you tried something new, just to see what would happen? When was the last time you took a risk, just to see where it would take you? If you can't remember, then it's time to start exploring. Because the rewards of exploration are so much greater than the risks. And the world is waiting for you to discover them.

A lot of my messaging so far has been specifically for seemingly ‚Äúgrand‚Äù life choices. But this same exploration philosophy applies for any decision or problem you have - however big or small. If you‚Äôre in a negotiation, explore creative options (e.g. swap teams to put them into each others‚Äô shoes to solve people problems, look for ways to combine forces rather than remain competitors, look at differences as an opportunity to create value). I do this all the time when I'm stuck.


üçÉ   Dynamic

One thing to be aware of is that the ‚Äúoptimal‚Äù amount of exploration is not a static value. It is a dynamic one. In poker, there is a concept of game theory optimal, or GTO, which is the absolute best strategy a player can play. Poker is a complex game, and we don‚Äôt know what the GTO strategy looks like exactly (we have some approximations with solvers and AI). In poker tournaments, the GTO is actually dynamic because the variables of the game constantly change minute to minute: the blinds, the chip stack sizes, the players you sit with, etc. The best tournament poker players are very exploitative and able to quickly adapt with the constantly shifting dynamics of the game. Cash games, which are more common in your typical frat and casino, have a more static GTO strategy since the variables are more fixed.

In general, your ‚Äúoptimal‚Äù amount of exploring decreases with more ‚Äúfixed‚Äù responsibilities. When you have kids, for example, you can‚Äôt simply ignore raising them. This is why younger people can take on more risk (e.g. launch a startup). Other examples of fixed responsibilities could be religious duties, a full-time job, relationships, etc. If you‚Äôve found something truly special, something that ‚Äúcalls‚Äù to you, you will naturally spend less time exploring ‚Äî don‚Äôt lie to yourself though! But something like classes are not fixed responsibilities, especially if you find them boring. It‚Äôs ok to spend less time, get by with a ‚Äúworse‚Äù grade, and spend all the other time on things you value more.

Because your life variables are changing so often, it‚Äôs hard to evaluate what the right amount of exploring is. Is it trying to learn one or two new topics this week? Is it five or six? Unfortunately, I believe there is no better answer than to just play around! You‚Äôll have to be brutally honest with yourself. Do you actually think that trying one more class or testing out one more activity is past your exploratory limit? Or is it because of your inherent bias towards inaction or fear of other‚Äôs judgement? Another way to find out is to err on the side of exploring too much. You won‚Äôt know your limit until you reach it!


üé≤   Average

Most exploring will end up with you realizing that you don‚Äôt like the ‚Äúthing‚Äù. But that‚Äôs not a sign of failure like what most people would think. If you look at the median or mode result, exploration looks like a scam. Why bother exploring if most of the time you‚Äôll find out you didn‚Äôt like something?

But people fail to account for two things. First, the results of exploration have high variance. Imagine if I rolled a six-sided die where you lose $1 on numbers 1-5 and won $1000 on a 6. You would obviously want to play this game even though the median and mode result is a loss of $1. That‚Äôs because the average result is that you earn about $166 dollars per roll. This sounds extreme, but that‚Äôs exactly like the payout for exploration. Most of the time, you‚Äôll lose a little bit of time (1 day? 1 week? 1 month?), but sometimes you‚Äôll discover something amazing that you‚Äôll devote a lot of time to (1 year? 3 years? 5-10 years?). The order of magnitudes is completely different. So looking at the average result makes it much more clear why exploration is so crucial and taking an absurd number of shots matters a lot. Bump the number of shots that you think you should take in the next year by at least an order of magnitude, and you‚Äôll be closer to what you should be at.

Second, the ‚Äúpayouts‚Äù are not as clearly defined as ‚Äúlosing $1‚Äù or ‚Äúwinning $1000‚Äù. They also aren‚Äôt necessarily monetary. Sometimes you‚Äôll learn that you hated an activity/subject, but learned an interesting key principle or two from it. Does that mean you ‚Äúlost‚Äù? Even though you learned something cool? A lot of us are quick to assume that we know the value of something, not accounting for the fact that life has a lot of uncertainty in it.

I‚Äôm even willing to go as far as to say that if you‚Äôre exploring ‚Äúcorrectly‚Äù (i.e. maximizing for learning rather than results: for soccer, learning under good coaches rather than doing pointless drills by yourself or for math, taking high value, inspirational classes rather than studying old textbooks that drill memorization into your head), then you‚Äôre never going to get a negative value dice roll. The younger you are, the more you should maximize for learning and increasing your ‚Äúpotential‚Äù. Explore stuff where you think by the end of the day, you‚Äôll learn a lot from it even if you end up not liking it. While this might sound obvious, you are probably lying to yourself about which things will actually increase your potential. The amount of bias that‚Äôs inherent in us, whether that‚Äôs our peer‚Äôs judgement of us, parental influence, or lure of money, severely clouds our judgement. It‚Äôs part of the reason why you see so many of the most brilliant students in the world funnel themselves into niche jobs where you‚Äôll make a boat load of money, even if it doesn‚Äôt make anyone‚Äôs lives better except the pockets of the elite and themselves (there are, of course, exceptions to this, but they are rarer). There are so many things out there that many are ignoring. The world is so much more infinite than the current tunnel vision a majority of society has.
